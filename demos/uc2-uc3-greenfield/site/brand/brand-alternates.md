# Brand alternates — not picked for this demo

> The UC2 + UC3 greenfield demo locked **HOLLER & HYMN** (see `BRAND.md`). The two directions below were the runners-up — both validated, both Asheville-rooted, both built off outlaw-country / honky-tonk tension. Saved here because either could carry a future demo, a sibling product, or an A/B exploration if the picked direction ever wobbles.

---

## Alternate B — WHIPPOORWILL

**One-line:** Botanical liqueurs from Madison County for the night shift. The whippoorwill calls at dusk and that's the brand.

**Tension flavor:** Pure roadhouse / Saturday-night coding. Less Sunday-morning to push against than Holler & Hymn, more about the bar at last call. Lighter on tension; heavier on single iconic image.

**Why it ranked second:** Strongest single-word brand-name elegance. The whippoorwill is the canonical Appalachian night bird ("Hear that lonesome whippoorwill" — Hank Williams). One bird, one word, one image — easier to render visually than Holler & Hymn, simpler to remember after one mention. But the tension move is shallower: no Sunday-morning half. If we ever want a simpler, more iconic sibling brand, this is the one.

**Maker — Junior (no last name on the bottle):**

> "The whippoorwill calls for one reason. To find another whippoorwill. That's about all the philosophy I can offer for what I do. I forage with a headlamp and I write product names on cocktail napkins. The herbs come from Madison County, the bottles come from a glass guy in Knoxville, the still I built myself in 2019. If you find one of these in a bar in Asheville, the bartender probably knows me, and the bartender probably knows what's in it. The labels are painted by my cousin who does jukebox cabinets. — Junior"

**Three named products:**

- **LONG NIGHT** — slow, deep amaro for after last call.
- **CALL & ANSWER** — paired vermouth + bitter aperitif. (The bird's two-note call.)
- **HEADLIGHT** — citrus-bitter low-ABV. (The drive home you can't actually make.)

**Palette:**

```css
--cricket-navy:    #0e0d12;  /* woods at midnight */
--headlight:       #f6c84c;  /* jukebox bulb amber */
--taillight:       #d22a1c;  /* neon OPEN sign */
--pearlsnap:       #5a4938;  /* faded denim hem brown */
--napkin:          #e6dcc7;  /* cocktail-napkin cream */
--motel-cyan:      #41bfb0;  /* motel pool at 2am, accent only */
```

**Typography:** hand-painted Western slab + neon-tube script for product names + condensed sans for labels.

**Page list:** `home / the maker / the night (manifesto) / the bottle (products) / the bird (name story) / where to find / the still / contact`

**Imagery direction (one paragraph):** deep blue-hour Appalachian forest, jukebox-lit bar interior at last call, hand-painted matchbook art, vintage neon signage in dim weather, a single whippoorwill silhouette, mason jars in headlight glow, cocktail napkin with handwriting. Slightly more cinematic / noir than Holler & Hymn's almanac-and-tent grammar.

**When this brand would win over Holler & Hymn:** if the demo wanted a single-image-driven brand (one bird, one word) instead of a duality-driven brand (holler & hymn). Lower copy demand, higher visual demand.

## Alternate C — HIGH LONESOME

**One-line:** Hand-made spirits sung into being from a barn ten miles from anywhere. Bottled bluegrass.

**Tension flavor:** Reverent, not irreverent. Bill Monroe coined "high lonesome" for the bluegrass sound — the brand sits closer to mountain-music / heritage-craft than to bar/jukebox grammar. Drinks for a Tennessee Williams short story, not a Friday-night roadhouse.

**Why it ranked third:** Highest single-word brand-name elegance, but lowest tension-conviction. "High Lonesome" reads as meditative and tasteful, which is exactly the *opposite* of the reference brand's load-bearing move. The risk: it slides into Kinfolk-Appalachian, the well-trodden default for "artisanal Southern." Saved here for a future moment where we *want* a quieter brand — e.g. a sister brand to Holler & Hymn aimed at a more meditative occasion, or a non-alcoholic line that doesn't need the bar-grammar of A or B.

**Maker — Sadie:**

> "There's a thing in old-time music called the high lonesome sound. It's not sad exactly. It's the sound of one voice in a wide place. That's what I'm trying to put in a bottle. The herbs grow in those wide places — the gaps, the balds, the deep coves — and I work in a small barn ten miles from anywhere. I sing while I work. I think it goes in. — Sadie"

**Three named products:**

- **BLUE RIDGE BLUE** — cobalt-tinged aperitif (butterfly-pea flower).
- **THE GAP** — sharp, bright high-elevation amaro.
- **DEEP COVE** — dark, sweet cordial.

**Palette:**

```css
--ridge-dusk:      #21323a;  /* Blue Ridge dusk */
--fiddle-bone:     #d8c19a;  /* bone fiddle ivory */
--hymnbook-blood:  #9c5a3a;  /* old hymnbook oxblood */
--spruce:          #3a4f3d;  /* spruce dark */
--page-cream:      #e9e3d2;  /* hand-set page cream */
--hymnal-rule:     #c43935;  /* single hymnal-red rule, accent */
```

**Typography:** hand-set woodcut display + hymn-book serif body + almanac numerals.

**Page list:** `home / the song (maker) / the gaps (foraging map) / the still (process) / the bottle (products) / shop / contact`

**Imagery direction (one paragraph):** Blue Ridge mist, dawn light on a high bald, a fiddle on a windowsill, hand-set type proofs on a barn table, plant-press specimens, hand-bound notebook. Painterly and meditative; pull back from any roadhouse or neon imagery.

**When this brand would win over Holler & Hymn:** if the brief required a quieter, more reverent register — for instance a demo aimed at hospitality / hotel-spirits buyers rather than bar buyers. Or if Holler & Hymn ever felt too loud for the customer being addressed.

## What B and C share with A (the Asheville constants)

All three brands sit on the same set of place-and-product constants. Re-use whenever needed:

- **Region:** Western North Carolina / Madison-or-Buncombe County / 30–60 minutes from Asheville
- **Category:** small-batch botanical liqueurs (amari / bitters / cordials / aperitivi), all-natural, no synthetic aromas
- **Botanicals:** sourwood, staghorn sumac, sassafras, paw-paw, persimmon, ramps (spring-only), mountain mint, ginseng (sustainable), chicory, eastern juniper, magnolia bark, black walnut hull, muscadine grape
- **Maker model:** single named person, first-person voice, no corporate plural
- **Distribution:** main site = brand storytelling; shop = separate subdomain; catalog = downloadable PDF
