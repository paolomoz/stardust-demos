# Holler & Hymn — Brand definition

> Source document. Inputs to: Gemini (logo + imagery prompts), Stardust (`/stardust:direct` phrase + hand-edited DESIGN), and the UC2 + UC3 demo's on-screen artifacts. Keep this as the single source of truth — when in doubt, this file wins.

## Identity at a glance

**Name:** **HOLLER & HYMN**
**Tagline:** *Saturday night sin. Sunday morning hymn. Both in one bottle.*
**Category:** Small-batch Appalachian botanical liqueurs — amari, bitters, cordials, low-ABV aperitifs.
**Place:** Wolfpen Holler, Madison County, Western North Carolina — forty minutes outside Asheville.
**Maker:** Wendell Greene. Single-maker brand; everything hand-foraged, hand-distilled, hand-labeled within forty miles of his barn.

## The tension (the central brand move)

Two halves at full volume, sitting on top of each other:

- **The HOLLER** — outlaw, place-rooted, foraged, hand-made. The barn, the still, the woods, the moonshine inheritance, the pickup truck. Saturday-night side.
- **The HYMN** — heritage, ritual, language, restraint. The hymnal on the kitchen table, the Sunday-morning sermon, the revival tent. Sunday-morning side.

The brand is the **ampersand**: the holler and the hymn at the same time, refusing to choose. Every product, every line of copy, every visual element should sit on both sides at once. A bitter named for the offering plate. A cordial named for the dropout. A label hand-painted in a shed bought off a preacher.

This is the load-bearing move. Strip either half and the brand collapses:

- Pure outlaw / honky-tonk → it becomes costume.
- Pure heritage / hymnal → it becomes Etsy.

Both halves at full volume, both rooted in WNC, both rendered with conviction. That's the brand.

## The maker — Wendell Greene

A single person makes this brand. The maker IS the brand. The voice is first-person, never corporate plural.

**Origin paragraph** (the about-page anchor — use verbatim or as the voice reference):

> "I grew up in a hollow outside Asheville where my grandmother kept a still in the smokehouse and a hymnal on the kitchen table. We never thought of them as different things. The botanicals come from the same woods that grew the rhododendron my grandfather got married under. The recipes come from the same Sunday afternoons that grew the hymns. Everything in these bottles is foraged or grown within forty miles of my barn. Nothing is bought in a jug. The labels are painted by hand, by me, in a shed I bought off a dead preacher.
> — Wendell"

**Tone calibration:**

- Slow, plainspoken, declarative.
- Lists outweigh adjectives.
- Specifics outweigh adjectives twice over. ("Forty miles," not "local." "Painted in a shed I bought off a dead preacher," not "lovingly hand-painted.")
- Never apologetic. Never preening. Confident enough not to explain itself.
- Cheek lives in the product names and label art, not in the maker's voice — Wendell himself talks like he's tired and right.

## Voice & copy rules

### Do

- First-person singular. *I, my, me.*
- Plainspoken declaratives. Short sentences. Periods.
- Concrete nouns and proper place names.
- Process specifics ("forty miles," "from a to z," "in a shed").
- The occasional King James cadence in section headers ("Drink ye all of it") — restrained, not constant.
- Numbers that prove the literalism: forty miles, twenty-seven herbs, eleven labels painted by hand.

### Don't

- "We" / "our team" / "passionate" / "crafted" / "curated" / "purveyors" / "lifestyle." All forbidden.
- Adjective stacks. Three adjectives in a row is the brand collapsing.
- Hyperbole ("the finest," "unparalleled"). Wendell doesn't talk like that.
- Cocktail-jargon snobbery (no "molecular," no "aromatic compound profile").
- Apostrophes in product names trying to sound country ("Granny's", "Daddy's"). Costume.

### Voice anchors (lift these phrasings)

- "Made by hand. From a to z."
- "Foraged inside forty miles."
- "Nothing bought in a jug."
- "Twenty-seven herbs, eleven labels, one preacher's shed."
- "If you find one in a bar in Asheville, the bartender probably knows me."
- "Saturday night sin. Sunday morning hymn."

## The central wordplay (the irreverence engine)

The brand runs sustained wordplay on **revival / gospel / church / sin / salvation** language — applied to drinking products. This is the US-South equivalent of the load-bearing irreverence in the reference brand. Use sparingly enough that each instance lands; never every label, never every section.

Vocabulary surface to draw from:

| Sacred side | Profane side |
|---|---|
| revival, tent, sermon, hymn, hymnal | sin, backslider, dropout, no-show |
| pulpit, pew, altar, offering plate | jukebox, last call, headlight, tailgate |
| Sunday morning, the lord's work | Saturday night, the devil's hour |
| pass the plate, take and drink | take a slug, pour one out |
| deacon, brother, sister, elder | outlaw, hellraiser, ne'er-do-well |
| amen, blessing, salvation | sin, redemption, the long way home |

The brand never mocks faith. It plays on the *language* of faith because that language is in the bones of the place — Wendell grew up with both sides. The wordplay is affectionate, not contemptuous. (The reference brand gets the Catholic-mass jokes the same way: from inside the culture, not above it.)

## Visual identity

### Palette (load-bearing)

```css
--hymnal-black:    #1a1410;  /* old leather Bible, hymnal-leather brown-black */
--barn-rust:       #7e1d12;  /* oxidized iron, old red-barn paint */
--gilt-gold:       #d4a45c;  /* gilded hymnal edges, brass collection plate */
--rhody-green:     #3e5a3e;  /* rhododendron leaf, deep WNC forest green */
--bulletin-cream:  #e8d8b8;  /* Sunday-bulletin paper, sun-bleached */
--hellfire-neon:   #f04a26;  /* jukebox neon, accent only — never primary */
```

**Rules:**

- `--hymnal-black` and `--bulletin-cream` are the two primary surfaces. Most of the brand lives here.
- `--barn-rust` is the load-bearing accent. Hand-lettering, ampersands, primary marks.
- `--gilt-gold` is reserved for hymnal-edge moments — gilded letters, decorative rules.
- `--rhody-green` is the only "nature" color and only for botanical-context blocks.
- `--hellfire-neon` is the irreverence accent. Use *sparingly* — a jukebox bulb on a Sunday bulletin. Never on a hymnal-black surface dominating a section.

### Typography

Three-typeface system:

1. **Display — hand-lettered revival-tent.** All-caps, rough-edged, looks like it was hand-painted on plywood in the 1940s. Strong stroke variation. Reference faces: *Saloon-style display*, *Knockout*, *Camp Type*, *hand-lettered display script with rough condition*.
2. **Body — hymnal serif.** Old-style serif with the proportions of an old hymnbook page. Medium tracking. Reference faces: *Hoefler Text-style hymnal serif*, *Bookman*, *Cycles*, *Sentinel*.
3. **Numerals + small caps + label callouts — almanac sans / typewriter.** For dates, ABV, product codes, footer details. Reference faces: *IBM Selectric*, *Courier with character*, *almanac sans*.

Headlines use the hand-lettered display in `--barn-rust` on `--bulletin-cream` (Sunday-bulletin mode) or in `--bulletin-cream` on `--hymnal-black` (revival-tent mode). The two modes alternate by section.

### Imagery direction (for Gemini prompts)

The image system has **three lanes**. Mix them, don't blend them.

**Lane 1 — Place / forage / process (the holler half).**
Photographic-realistic, golden-hour or blue-hour Western North Carolina mountain landscapes. Mist in the hollows. Sourwood blossoms. Mason jars of dried sumac and mountain mint on a barn shelf. A copper still in dim light. Pearl-snap shirt, faded denim, foraging hands holding ramps. Worn wood, weathered tin roofs, hand-painted plywood signs. Saturated rust-red and forest-green palette; never desaturated luxe.

*Gemini prompt seed:* "golden hour, misty Western North Carolina mountain holler, hand-painted plywood sign reading [---], close-up texture, photographic realism, rust-red barn paint, forest-green rhododendron, no people unless specified, no Adobe Stock luxe-craft feel."

**Lane 2 — Hymnal / revival / heritage (the hymn half).**
Photographic stills of an old leather hymnal on a worn wood kitchen table. Brass-edged pages. A handwritten recipe slipped inside. A revival-tent silhouette at dusk. A Sunday bulletin's typography enlarged. A folding chair, a wooden pew. The objects of Southern church culture, photographed with respect — never costume, never mockery.

*Gemini prompt seed:* "still-life photograph, worn leather hymnal open on kitchen table, brass page edges, handwritten note tucked in pages, soft north window light, dust visible in air, very low saturation except for the leather brown and brass gold."

**Lane 3 — Label art / jukebox / bar (the wordplay surface).**
Hand-painted bottle labels, irregular registration, dripping ink. Mid-century jukebox typography. Matchbook art. Tent-revival flyers. Faded country-music album sleeves. Slightly punk in execution, despite the heritage subject. This is the lane where the irreverence is loudest.

*Gemini prompt seed:* "hand-painted vintage label art, 1950s revival-tent flyer aesthetic, rough registration, dripping rust-red ink on cream paper, all-caps hand-lettered display type reading [PASS THE PLATE], single decorative line rule, no gradient, no digital perfection."

**Cross-lane don'ts:**

- No Kinfolk-magazine luxe-minimal beige-and-cream styling. That is the wrong brand.
- No Etsy-cottagecore floral framing.
- No banjo-on-the-porch costume staging. The holler is real; we don't dress it up.
- No purple/magenta. The palette is rust + cream + black + gold + green. Stay in it.

### Logo direction (for Gemini prompts)

The brand mark is an **ampersand-centered seal**. The "&" between "HOLLER" and "HYMN" is the load-bearing graphic — it is the brand. Visual options to test in Gemini:

1. **Circular seal.** "HOLLER ✦ HYMN" arcing top-and-bottom of a circle, ornament-centered. Reads like a sermon-program header or a fruit-crate label.
2. **Stacked wordmark.** "HOLLER" / "&" (oversized, hand-painted, rust-red) / "HYMN" stacked vertically, with a single rule between. Reads like a tent-revival poster.
3. **Apothecary panel.** "HOLLER & HYMN" in a long horizontal panel, hand-lettered, with small almanac-sans subtext ("OF WOLFPEN HOLLER · MADISON CO. N.C."). Reads like an old apothecary cabinet drawer.

**Across all three:**

- Hand-lettered, never digital-perfect. Roughness is the brand.
- Rust-red on cream OR cream on hymnal-black, depending on context. Avoid full-color logos.
- The ampersand is enlarged or decorated — it carries the tension.
- Distressed/woodblock-print finish is encouraged; clean vector is not.

*Gemini prompt seed for the logo:*

> "Hand-lettered vintage logo for a small-batch Appalachian liqueur maker. Brand name: HOLLER & HYMN. Style: 1940s revival-tent poster meets 1920s apothecary cabinet drawer. Round seal or vertical stacked layout. Oversized rough-painted ampersand at center, rust-red `#7e1d12` on Sunday-bulletin cream `#e8d8b8`. Hand-painted strokes, irregular ink density, slight registration drift, distressed wood-block print finish. No gradient, no digital vector look, no swooshes. Below the wordmark in small almanac-sans caps: 'OF WOLFPEN HOLLER · MADISON CO. NC'."

Generate 6–10 variations, pick a strong one, refine.

## Product family

Small-batch botanical spirits, all made in WNC from hand-foraged Appalachian botanicals. Categories:

- **Amari** — bittersweet herbal digestifs, the brand's spine.
- **Bitters** — small-format cocktail bitters.
- **Cordials** — sweet, fruit/flower-forward, low-to-medium ABV.
- **Aperitivi** — low-ABV pre-dinner drinks.

WNC botanicals to anchor recipe and copy: **sourwood blossom**, **staghorn sumac**, **sassafras root**, **paw-paw**, **wild persimmon**, **ramps** (spring-only), **mountain mint**, **wild ginseng** (sustainably), **chicory root**, **eastern juniper**, **magnolia bark**, **black walnut hull**, **muscadine grape**.

## Anti-patterns (what this brand is NOT)

- ❌ Kinfolk / Cereal Magazine luxe-minimal-craft. Beige, sage, charcoal, generous white space, serif italic. **This is the dominant default for "artisanal" — refuse it.**
- ❌ Brooklyn industrial-craft (Heath Ceramics, Apothéke). Edison-bulb, subway-tile, Helvetica-on-blackboard. Wrong era, wrong region.
- ❌ Wes Anderson costume-craft. Symmetrical pastel framing, Futura, Lake Como vibe. (This is what the reference brand happens to draft on — *we deliberately do not.*)
- ❌ Etsy folk-craft. Hand-drawn fern in dusty rose. Cute. We are not cute.
- ❌ Generic country/farm signage. "Y'all means all" doormat energy. We are not signage.
- ❌ Modern craft-beer label vernacular (illustration-heavy can wraps, all-caps fashion-foundry display). Adjacent market, wrong genre.

The closest *cultural reference points* (without imitating execution): old WNC fruit-crate labels, 1940s revival-tent posters, vintage Appalachian state-fair signage, the typography of *The Foxfire Book*, country-music album sleeves from the early 1970s outlaw era, hand-painted plywood roadside signs you still see on US-25 north of Asheville.
